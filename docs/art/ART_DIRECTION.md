# Art Direction - Mallcore Sim

## Overview

Mallcore Sim is a stylized 3D retail simulator inspired by 2000s mall culture.
The visual direction prioritizes warmth, readability, and nostalgia over realism.
Think cozy, inviting, and slightly exaggerated -- never photoreal.

## Reference Aesthetic

The target sits somewhere between Overcooked's visual clarity and Unpacking's
cozy attention to detail. Environments should feel like a warm memory of hanging
out at the mall, not a photographic recreation of one.

## Tone

- Nostalgic: early-2000s mall vibes, food courts, anchor stores, kiosks
- Inviting: players should want to spend time in the space
- Slightly humorous: exaggerated product labels, quirky store names, playful signage
- Never cynical or mean-spirited about retail culture

## Color Palette

| Zone             | Direction                                                  |
|------------------|------------------------------------------------------------|
| Backgrounds      | Muted warm tones (soft beige, light taupe, dusty rose)     |
| Store fixtures   | Medium saturation naturals (wood, metal, laminate reads)   |
| Products         | High saturation, punchy colors for shelf readability       |
| Lighting         | Warm whites and soft ambers, no harsh cool tones           |
| UI               | Clean, high-contrast, distinct from world palette          |
| Accent/highlight | Teal or coral pops for interactive elements and callouts   |

Backgrounds stay quiet so products and interactive elements pop. Products are the
stars -- they need to read instantly on shelves.

## Readability Principles

- Readable shelf and product silhouettes are critical. Players must identify
  items at a glance during gameplay.
- Strong color and material separation between store fixtures, products, and UI.
  No two categories should compete for visual attention.
- Silhouette test: every product category should be identifiable in solid black.
- Avoid noisy textures on shelves and fixtures that compete with product reads.

## Scale and Proportions

- Slightly exaggerated proportions throughout. Nothing is 1:1 real-world scale.
- Products are approximately 1.2x real-world scale for shelf readability.
- Characters use stylized proportions (larger heads, simplified hands).
- Store fixtures lean chunky and solid rather than thin and delicate.
- Doorways, aisles, and counters are wider than real life for camera clarity.

## Lighting

- Primary: warm overhead fluorescent panels (soft white, slight amber tint).
- Accent: colored spotlights on product displays and featured areas.
- Ambient: gentle fill to avoid harsh shadows, keep the mood cozy.
- No dynamic time-of-day for interiors. Consistent, controlled retail lighting.
- Skylight areas and food courts can have slightly cooler, brighter fill.
- Neon signage provides localized color splashes, especially in hallways.

## Camera Considerations

- The game is desktop-first with an isometric or slightly tilted top-down camera.
- Art must read well at the default zoom level. Avoid small details that vanish.
- Floor patterns and tiles should be subtle, not distracting at camera distance.
- Wall art and signage should be legible but not overwhelming.

## Materials Philosophy

- Flat-ish shading with subtle gradients. Avoid heavy PBR roughness/metallic.
- Hand-painted texture influence is welcome but not required.
- Specular highlights kept minimal -- just enough to sell material types.
- Transparency used sparingly (glass storefronts, drink cups).

## What to Avoid

- Photorealism or uncanny valley territory
- Desaturated, gritty, or "realistic" color grading
- Overly complex geometry that does not read at gameplay camera distance
- Visual noise on fixture surfaces that competes with product silhouettes
- Cool-toned lighting as a default (reserve for specific narrative moments)

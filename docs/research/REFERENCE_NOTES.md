# Reference Notes

Games, media, and cultural references informing mallcore-sim's design. These are research observations, not binding design decisions.

---

## Reference Games

### Recettear: An Item Shop's Tale (2007/2010)

The most direct inspiration. A JRPG where you run an item shop.

**What to learn**:
- The buy-low-sell-high loop is inherently satisfying
- Haggling with customers adds tension to every sale
- Dungeon crawling (sourcing inventory) makes the shop loop richer
- Character relationships with repeat customers create emotional investment
- The "just one more day" hook is powerful

**What to avoid**:
- The debt repayment timer adds stress that conflicts with our "cozy" pillar
- Dungeon gameplay splits focus -- we want the store itself to be the whole game
- Anime aesthetic doesn't fit 2000s mall vibe

### Moonlighter (2018)

Action-roguelike shopkeeping hybrid.

**What to learn**:
- The pricing discovery mechanic (customers react to prices in real-time) is brilliant and tactile
- Watching customers browse and seeing their mood shift based on price is great feedback
- Shop upgrades feel meaningful (bigger shop, display cases, decorations)
- The journal that tracks item price ranges rewards player knowledge

**What to avoid**:
- The combat/dungeon half isn't relevant to us
- Item variety felt thin for a pure shop game
- Customer types were limited

### Shoppe Keep (2015) / Shoppe Keep 2 (2018)

First-person medieval shopkeeping sim.

**What to learn**:
- Physical item placement (putting things on shelves yourself) is satisfying
- Shoplifters add tension and require attention
- Store cleanliness as a mechanic (sweeping floors) adds routine tasks that feel grounding
- The 3D store-walk perspective makes the space feel real

**What to avoid**:
- Repetitive without enough progression hooks
- Customer AI was too simple (beeline to item, buy, leave)
- Lacked the collector/rarity dimension that gives items meaning

### Game Dev Tycoon (2012)

Business simulation with progression and era-based content.

**What to learn**:
- Progression through eras keeps the game fresh (directly applicable: mall eras, product generations)
- Simple mechanics with emergent complexity from their interaction
- The research/unlock tree gives long-term goals
- Review scores as feedback (analogous to our reputation system)

**What to avoid**:
- Purely abstract -- no physical space to inhabit
- Menu-driven gameplay lacks the tactile quality we want

### Mall Tycoon (2002) / Mall Tycoon 2 (2003)

Top-down mall management sim.

**What to learn**:
- The fantasy of managing a mall is appealing to our target audience
- Store variety and customer routing between stores creates emergent behavior
- The 2000s mall aesthetic is exactly our target

**What to avoid**:
- Macro-scale management (dozens of stores) loses the personal touch
- We want to be IN the store, not above it
- Shallow individual store simulation

### Wilmot's Warehouse (2019)

Puzzle game about organizing a warehouse.

**What to learn**:
- The pure satisfaction of organizing items is a game in itself
- Player-defined organization systems (where you put things matters to you, not the game)
- Visual density of items in a space creates a cozy, full feeling
- Minimal UI, physical interaction with space

## 2000s Mall Culture References

These are the real-world stores and experiences that inform the game's atmosphere and store types.

### GameStop / EB Games
- Pre-owned game wall with price stickers
- Trade-in culture (not implementing trades at launch, but the aesthetic)
- The glass case with expensive items behind the register
- Strategy guide rack, magazine display
- "Reserve your copy" posters

### Blockbuster Video
- Blue and yellow color scheme, specific carpet smell
- New Release wall with dramatic cover art facing out
- The walk-around browse, reading the back of cases
- "Be Kind, Rewind" stickers
- Candy and popcorn at checkout
- Late fee drama

### Hot Topic
- Dark walls, loud music, packed merchandise
- Represents the "edgy" end of mall culture
- Band tees, patches, pins -- collector/display items
- Reference for the card shop's younger customer demographic

### Spencer's Gifts
- Novelty items, gag gifts, lava lamps
- The "weird stuff" store -- reference for consumer electronics gadgets
- Impulse buy culture

### Sports Authority / Champs
- Jersey displays, autographed memorabilia in cases
- Card packs at the checkout counter
- Team-branded everything

### FYE (For Your Entertainment)
- CDs, DVDs, pop culture merch
- Listening stations for CDs
- The "everything entertainment" store -- overlaps with several of our store types

### Card/Comic Shops (Local)
- Tournament tables in the back
- Binder singles behind the counter
- Booster box towers
- The regulars who hang out all day
- Hand-written price tags, condition grades

## Aesthetic Research Notes

### Lighting
- Mall stores of this era had harsh fluorescent overheads mixed with warm spotlights on displays
- The contrast between bright store interiors and dimmer mall corridors is iconic
- Neon open signs, backlit menu boards, illuminated display cases

### Sound
- Mall ambiance: distant crowd murmur, muzak, air conditioning hum
- Store-specific: register beeps, plastic bag rustling, game demo audio, sports commentary
- The specific sound of a card pack being opened (crinkling foil)

### Materials and Textures
- Industrial carpet (usually dark blue, dark red, or grey)
- Laminate wood shelving
- Glass display cases with sliding doors
- Pegboard walls with hook displays
- Drop ceiling tiles with fluorescent panels
- Chrome fixtures, metal grid displays

### Signage
- Hand-written price tags and "SALE" signs
- Printed category labels on shelf edges
- Poster-heavy walls (movie posters, game art, sports heroes)
- Neon "OPEN" signs, light-up brand logos
- "PLEASE ASK FOR ASSISTANCE WITH ITEMS IN THE DISPLAY CASE"

---

## Key Takeaway

The best retail sim reference games prove that the core buy/sell/display loop is inherently fun. What they often lack is depth in the items themselves -- items are interchangeable revenue units. mallcore-sim's differentiator is that items have identity: condition, rarity, collector value, and cultural context. The 2000s mall setting provides the atmosphere and specificity that makes browsing feel like time travel.
